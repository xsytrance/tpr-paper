# Worked Example Transition: C → D (Pneumatic-Tire Safety)

## Change

The safety bicycle (Rover-type diamond frame, solid rubber tires, 1885–1887) was fitted with pneumatic inflatable tires starting in 1888 (Dunlop patent). This replaced a harsh, high-rolling-resistance surface with a cushioned, lower-friction one. The modification affected only the wheel subsystem but changed the rider's experience substantially.

## Objective Pressures

- **Comfort improvement** – Pneumatic rubber absorbs road shocks, reducing fatigue on long or rough rides. Evidence: Dunlop's demonstration with racer Willie Hume (1889), contemporary accounts in *Wheeling* Magazine and The History Press. (Direct; confidence high)
- **Speed/performance increase** – Lower rolling resistance made cycling faster. Evidence: race results following Dunlop's introduction; engineering expectation. (Mixed direct/inferred; confidence medium)

## Subjective Pressures

- **Reduced safety anxieties** – Public health concerns about cycling (“bicycle face” debates in 1895 newspapers) show that perceived danger was salient. Pneumatic tires were marketed and perceived as healthier, mitigating those anxieties. Evidence: Readex historical newspapers (*Boston Daily Advertiser* quoting *St. James’s Budget*), *Wheeling* Magazine commentary. (Direct contemporary discussion; confidence medium)
- **Social acceptability for women** – Women's cycling expanded in the 1890s partly because a smoother ride made it more compatible with contemporary notions of feminine propriety and physical well-being. Evidence: modern cultural study (GMTMA) analyzing 19th-century advertisements that framed bicycles as suitable for women by emphasizing comfort and safety. (Mixed—scholarship directly states connection; primary ad corpus not fully reproduced here; confidence medium)
- **Aspirational modernity** – Pneumatic-tired safeties were positioned as cutting-edge, signaling personal progressiveness. Evidence: tone of *Wheeling* Magazine praise. (Inferred; confidence low)

## Uncertainty

- **Known:** Pneumatic tires demonstrably improved comfort and speed; they were widely adopted by the early 1890s.
- **Inferred:** That comfort reduction directly caused the explosion of women's cycling; that pneumatic tires dampened “bicycle face” health anxieties (correlated but not experimentally proven).
- **Ambiguous:** Relative importance of objective vs. subjective drivers; whether marketing led perception or reflected it.
