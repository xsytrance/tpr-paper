# Lineage Narrative (Clean)

This narrative presents the four-commit safety bicycle lineage (1876–1892) as a structured evolutionary sequence. Each commit represents a distinct, source-backed design state.

## Commit A: Lawson Lever Safety (1876–1878)

Harry John Lawson patented a lever-driven bicycle in 1876 (UK 2649), explicitly using the term “safety.” The machine had a low frame, smaller wheels than a high-wheeler, and hand/foot levers for propulsion. Weight was ~65.5 lb. It was produced in small numbers but not widely adopted. Objective pressures: improved stability (lower center of gravity) and mechanical simplicity (lever drive). Subjective pressure: the “safety” label itself as marketing rhetoric.

## Commit B: Lawson Bicyclette (1879–1884)

Lawson patented a chain-driven rear-wheel design in 1879 (UK 3924), known as the Bicyclette. This replaced levers with a chain, improving drivetrain efficiency. The front wheel remained smaller than the rear. Produced by Rudge in limited numbers. Objective pressures: increased efficiency (performance) and manufacturing refinement (inferred). No subjective pressures evidenced.

## Commit C: Rover Safety (1885–1887)

John Kemp Starley’s Rover Company (Coventry) produced the first widely successful safety bicycle in 1885. Key features: equal-sized wheels (26–30”), diamond frame, rear chain drive, solid rubber tires. This geometry greatly improved stability and simplified manufacturing. Objective pressures: safety (stability), cost reduction, manufacturability. Subjective pressures: perceived safety (explicit “safety” advertising) and fashion/modernity signaling (inferred from ad tone).

## Commit D: Pneumatic-Tire Safety (1888–1892)

John Boyd Dunlop’s pneumatic tire (1888) was fitted to safety bicycles, replacing solid rubber. The inflatable tube absorbed shocks, reduced fatigue, and lowered rolling resistance. Adoption was rapid during the 1890s cycling boom. Objective pressures: comfort and speed. Subjective pressures: reduced safety anxieties (evidenced by “bicycle face” press debate), expanded social acceptability for women (based on cultural analysis of ads), and aspirational modernity (inferred).

## Subcomponent: Tire

- **tire_solid (1876–1887):** Simple solid rubber.
- **tire_pneumatic_introduction (1888+):** Inflatable tube—comfort revolution with both objective and subjective selection signals.
