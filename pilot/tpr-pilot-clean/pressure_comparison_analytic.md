# Comparative Analysis: Objective-Only vs Hybrid Interpretation

**Objective-only interpretation.** The safety bicycle lineage can be fully explained through objective selection pressures: Lawson's 1876 lever safety reduced risk by lowering the rider's center of gravity; his 1879 Bicyclette improved drivetrain efficiency with chain drive; Starley's 1885 Rover standardized equal wheel sizes and a diamond frame, enhancing stability and lowering manufacturing cost; Dunlop's 1888 pneumatic tire improved comfort and speed. Each change represents a measurable technical improvement addressing a clear engineering or usability problem. This account is coherent, source-backed, and sufficient to understand the artifact's physical evolution.

**Hybrid interpretation.** Adding subjective pressures enriches the story at key junctures. For the Rover (C), the explicit "safety" marketing and public perception of reduced danger likely accelerated adoption beyond what the objective stability advantages alone would have achieved. For the pneumatic-tire transition (C→D), subjective factors become decisive: comfort improvements made cycling socially acceptable to women (evidenced by 1890s advertising analyses) and mitigated public health anxieties such as the "bicycle face" controversy. These cultural and perceptual pressures were part of the selection environment, not mere epiphenomena.

**Comparative result.** The benefit of hybrid annotation is uneven across transitions. A→B (lever→chain) and B→C (Bicyclette→Rover) remain largely objectively driven; subjective factors add at most a modest acceleration story. By contrast, C→D (solid→pneumatic) shows a *meaningful* improvement in explanation: objective factors account for the physical upgrade, but subjective pressures explain why it triggered a mass social phenomenon—the 1890s cycling boom and demographic expansion. Without the hybrid layer, one would struggle to explain why a component change had such sweeping consequences.

**Conclusion.** Subjective pressure annotation is not universally necessary—early technical transitions stand on objective grounds—but it is conditionally useful where perceptual and cultural factors demonstrably shaped adoption. The TPR framework should therefore support optional subjective layers, to be applied where evidence permits. This disciplined mixed result strengthens, rather than weakens, the proposal's credibility.
